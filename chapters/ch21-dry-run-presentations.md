## Why Rehearsal Matters More Than You Think

There is a moment that every presenter knows — and dreads. It comes approximately ninety seconds into a live demonstration when something on the screen does not behave the way it did during the last ten practice runs. The cursor hovers. The prompt returns something unexpected. The loading spinner becomes a philosophical statement about the nature of time.

For most presentations, this moment is merely awkward. For an AI workflow demo in front of banking executives, it can feel like a five-alarm fire.

Rehearsal is the only thing standing between you and that fire.

This session — Week 7, Session B — is not a lecture. It is a full dress rehearsal. Every participant will stand up, deliver their complete ten-minute project presentation to their peers, and receive structured feedback before the Showcase. The room is safe. The audience is friendly. The consequences are zero.

That changes next week.

But here is what experienced presenters know that beginners do not: the goal of a dry run is not to perform perfectly. It is to find every crack in the floor before the real audience walks in. The rough edges you discover today are gifts. The ones you miss are liabilities.

This chapter gives you the frameworks, coaching tips, checklists, and battle-tested strategies to turn your dry-run session into the most productive ninety minutes of this entire course — and to set you up not just for a great Showcase, but for a long career as an AI champion inside Helm Bank.

## Part One: The Architecture of a Great AI Presentation

### What Makes AI Demos Different

Presenting an AI workflow is categorically different from presenting a quarterly report or a project update. Three forces are at work that do not exist in conventional business presentations.

**First: The output is non-deterministic.** When you show a slide deck, slide seven looks exactly the same every time. When you run a Copilot prompt live, the response changes. Sometimes dramatically. This is not a bug in your presentation — it is a feature of generative AI — but it requires a completely different psychological contract with your audience and a completely different preparation discipline.

**Second: The audience brings fear as well as curiosity.** Banking executives at Helm Bank are not a neutral audience. Some are genuinely excited about AI. Others are worried about compliance, job displacement, data security, and regulatory exposure. When you demonstrate an AI tool, you are not just showing a workflow — you are managing a room full of people who carry invisible agendas about what AI means for their world. Great AI presenters understand this and address it proactively.

**Third: The technology itself can become the subject.** This is subtle but important. In a conventional presentation, the technology (the laptop, the projector) is invisible. In an AI demo, the technology is the point — and that means the audience's attention is split between what you are saying and what they are watching on the screen. You have to choreograph both channels simultaneously.

Understanding these three forces is the foundation of every coaching tip that follows.

### The Ten-Minute Showcase Structure

Your presentation window is ten minutes, followed by five minutes of Q&A. That is not a lot of time. Here is how top-performing participants in previous cohorts have allocated it.

| Segment | Duration | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Hook + Problem Statement | 90 seconds | Grab attention, establish stakes |
| STAR-AI Framework | 5 minutes | Tell the story with structure |
| Live Demo or Demo Clip | 2 minutes | Show, don't just tell |
| Impact + Future Vision | 60 seconds | Land the ROI message |
| Call to Action | 30 seconds | Tell them what you want them to do |

Notice that the live demo — the thing most presenters spend 80% of their prep time on — occupies only two minutes of the ten. The story around the demo is what carries the presentation. The demo is evidence. The story is the argument.

## Part Two: The STAR-AI Framework

The single most powerful structural tool for presenting AI workflow projects is the STAR-AI framework. It is an evolution of the classic STAR interview technique (Situation, Task, Action, Result), adapted specifically for AI project storytelling in a corporate context.

```{figure} figures/ch21-star-ai-framework.png
:name: fig-star-ai
:alt: A five-segment wheel diagram showing the STAR-AI framework: Situation, Task, AI Approach, Results, with the Helm Bank logo at the center
:align: center
The STAR-AI framework gives your presentation a spine. Every minute of your ten-minute slot maps to one of these segments.
```

### S — Situation

The Situation segment answers one question: *What was the world like before your project?*

This is not a data dump. It is an emotional anchor. You want the audience — whether they work in compliance, relationship banking, trade finance, or operations — to nod their heads and think: *Yes, I know exactly what that feels like.*

**What to include in Situation:**
- A specific, vivid description of the workflow problem (not generic, not theoretical)
- The human cost: time spent, frustration experienced, errors made
- Context that makes the problem feel urgent at Helm Bank specifically

**What to avoid in Situation:**
- Industry-wide statistics that have nothing to do with Helm Bank
- Technical jargon that forces executives to translate before they can empathize
- More than 60–90 seconds — this segment sets the stage, it does not own the show

**Example — Strong Situation Statement:**

*"Every Monday morning, our trade finance team spends an average of two hours manually extracting data from incoming Letters of Credit documents — most of them arriving as PDFs in Spanish or Portuguese — and reformatting that data into our compliance intake forms. It is tedious, it is error-prone, and it happens every single week. On a busy week, two hours becomes four. When we are short-staffed, documents sit in a queue and our clients wait. That is the world we were working in six weeks ago."*

Notice what that statement does. It is specific (Letters of Credit, Monday mornings, Spanish and Portuguese). It quantifies without being overwhelming (two hours, four hours on busy weeks). And it ends on a human note — clients wait — that any executive can relate to.

**Example — Weak Situation Statement:**

*"AI adoption in the financial services industry has been growing rapidly. According to a McKinsey report, 78% of financial institutions are investing in AI. Helm Bank needs to stay competitive."*

This is generic, impersonal, and it positions the presenter as someone who read a report rather than someone who lived the problem.

---

### T — Task

The Task segment answers: *What specifically were you trying to accomplish?*

This is the moment where you define the scope of your project with precision. The Task statement should be tight enough that a stranger could understand exactly what problem you attacked, and broad enough that the solution you built feels proportionate.

**Task statement formula:**
> "My goal was to [specific action verb] the [specific process] so that [specific team or person] could [specific benefit], without [specific constraint or concern]."

**Example:**

*"My goal was to automate the initial extraction and categorization of data from incoming LC documents so that our trade finance analysts could begin compliance review in minutes instead of hours — without changing our existing approval workflow or touching any document that had not already been cleared by our intake team."*

The phrase "without" is critical. It signals to executives that you were thinking about guardrails and constraints from the beginning — not building recklessly.

---

### A — AI Approach

This is the heart of your presentation and the segment where most participants either win or lose the room.

The AI Approach segment answers: *What did you actually build, and why did you make the choices you made?*

Notice the second half of that question. It is not enough to show what you built. You must explain the reasoning behind your design choices. This is what separates a practitioner from a technician — and it is what impresses banking executives who are evaluating whether to trust you with expanded AI responsibility.

**Structure your AI Approach in three layers:**

**Layer 1 — Tool Selection Rationale**
Why did you choose Microsoft Copilot for this workflow? What alternatives did you consider? Why was Copilot the right fit for Helm Bank's environment?

*"I used Microsoft Copilot because it operates entirely within our Microsoft 365 tenant — that means the document data never leaves our environment, which was a non-negotiable requirement given our compliance obligations under BSA/AML guidelines."*

**Layer 2 — Workflow Design**
Walk through the workflow step by step, but at the level of logic, not clicks. Executives do not need to see every prompt — they need to understand the architecture of what you built.

*"The workflow has three stages. In stage one, Copilot reads the incoming PDF and extracts twelve specific data fields using a structured prompt I developed over three iterations. In stage two, it flags any fields where the extracted data does not match our validation rules — things like country codes, currency pairs, and document date logic. In stage three, it generates a pre-populated intake form that the analyst then reviews and approves."*

**Layer 3 — Key Design Decisions**
Highlight two or three choices you made that reflect deliberate thinking about risk, accuracy, or scale. This is where you demonstrate judgment, not just technical skill.

*"One decision I made early was to keep a human in the loop at every stage where a compliance determination is required. Copilot does the extraction and flagging, but it never makes a compliance decision. That stays with the analyst. I also built a confidence indicator into the output — if Copilot's extraction looks uncertain based on document quality or unusual formatting, it flags the field for manual review rather than guessing."*

---

### R — Results

The Results segment is where you land the plane. It answers: *What happened when you actually used this?*

Results must be specific, honest, and forward-looking. The three components of a strong Results statement are:

**Quantitative impact:** Numbers that a CFO or COO would recognize as meaningful. Time saved, error rates reduced, processing volume increased, staff hours reallocated.

**Qualitative impact:** The human experience of the change. How do your colleagues feel about the new workflow? What became possible that was not possible before?

**Future potential:** What would happen if this were scaled across the department, the region, or the bank? This is where you give executives a reason to invest in your idea beyond its current state.

**Example — Strong Results Statement:**

*"In three weeks of pilot use with two analysts on our team, the average time to complete initial LC data extraction dropped from 47 minutes per document to 8 minutes — a reduction of 83%. Error rates on field extraction fell significantly because the structured prompt catches formatting variations that human eyes miss when they are processing their twelfth document in a row. The analysts themselves told me they felt less exhausted at the end of the week. One of them said, 'I actually have time to think now.' If we scale this across our entire trade finance team of twelve analysts and apply it to the full volume of LC documents we process each month, we estimate annual time savings of approximately 1,400 hours — the equivalent of nearly one full-time position."*

That Results statement works because it layers numbers (83%, 1,400 hours), human experience ("I actually have time to think now"), and scale projection (full team of twelve, annual impact).

### Putting STAR-AI Together

The full STAR-AI arc, executed well, takes approximately five minutes and carries your audience through a complete emotional journey: recognition of a problem they understand, clarity about the goal, confidence in your approach, and excitement about the results.

## Part Three: Live Demo Coaching

### The Live Demo vs. Recorded Demo Decision

One of the first decisions you must make for your Showcase presentation is whether to run your Copilot demo live or to use a pre-recorded clip. There is no universally correct answer. The right choice depends on your workflow, your comfort level, and the reliability of the specific Copilot features you are demonstrating.

```{figure} figures/ch21-demo-decision-tree.png
:name: fig-demo-decision
:alt: A decision tree flowchart helping presenters choose between live demo and recorded demo based on workflow type and risk tolerance
:align: center
Use this decision framework to choose the right demo format for your presentation.
```

**Arguments for Live Demo:**
- Demonstrates real-time authenticity — the audience sees it working right now, in front of them
- Allows you to respond to audience interest — if someone leans forward when you are on a particular step, you can pause and explore
- Signals confidence — executives read a live demo as a sign that you trust your own work
- More memorable — live moments, including imperfect ones handled gracefully, stick in the memory

**Arguments for Recorded Demo:**
- Eliminates network and platform variability — the video always plays exactly the same way
- Allows precise editing — you can cut the twelve seconds where Copilot was loading and replace it with a voiceover
- Reduces cognitive load — you can focus entirely on narrating rather than simultaneously driving and speaking
- Essential when your workflow involves sensitive data that cannot be shown in a live environment

**The Hybrid Approach (Recommended for Most Participants):**

Use a recorded clip for the core workflow demonstration — the two-minute window in your ten-minute slot — but open and close with live Copilot interaction. Show the audience a quick live prompt at the start to establish that Copilot is real and running, then play your polished recorded clip for the detailed workflow, then return to live Copilot at the end to answer a specific question or show a variation.

This approach gives you the authenticity of live demo with the reliability of recorded demo. It also gives you a natural recovery path if something goes wrong with the live portion.

---

### Handling Live Copilot Variability

When you run Copilot live, the output will vary. This is a certainty, not a possibility. Copilot is a generative system — it is designed to produce contextually appropriate responses, not identical responses. Here is how to manage variability professionally.

**Preparation Strategy 1: Run your demo prompt twenty times before the Showcase.**

Not three times. Not five times. Twenty. Over the course of twenty runs, you will see the full range of output variation for your specific prompt. You will know which fields sometimes come back slightly differently formatted, which responses occasionally include extra context you did not ask for, and which parts of the workflow are rock-solid every time. This knowledge transforms variability from a threat into a known quantity.

**Preparation Strategy 2: Develop a "close enough" standard.**

For each step in your live demo, define in advance what "close enough" looks like. If Copilot formats a date as July 2, 2026 instead of 07/02/2026, is that a problem for your demo? Probably not. If Copilot occasionally adds a paragraph of explanation you did not ask for, can you narrate over it naturally? Probably yes. Knowing your "close enough" standard in advance prevents you from breaking stride over trivial variations.

**Preparation Strategy 3: Build narration bridges.**

A narration bridge is a sentence you prepare in advance that allows you to smoothly acknowledge a variation without losing your composure. Examples:

- *"You will notice Copilot added a bit of extra context there — that is actually a nice feature when you are working with less experienced team members who need more explanation."*
- *"The specific phrasing changes each time, but the key data elements are consistent — and those are what matter for our workflow."*
- *"That is a slightly different format than what I showed in my practice runs, but watch how it still maps perfectly to our intake form."*

These bridges do three things simultaneously: they acknowledge the variation (building trust with a sophisticated audience that notices everything), they reframe the variation as a feature rather than a flaw, and they keep your narrative momentum moving forward.

### The "What If It Fails" Protocol

Despite perfect preparation, live technology sometimes fails in ways that cannot be predicted or prevented. A network blip. A platform outage. An authentication error. A prompt that, on this particular morning, returns something completely unhelpful.

Every presenter who works with live AI demos needs a failure protocol — a pre-planned sequence of responses that kicks in automatically when things go sideways.

**The Helm Bank Failure Protocol has four steps:**

**Step 1 — The Calm Pivot (0 to 10 seconds after failure)**

Do not panic. Do not apologize more than once. Do not narrate your own distress. Instead, take one breath, make direct eye contact with your audience, and say:

*"Technology has opinions — and today it is expressing one. Let me switch to the recorded version I have ready."*

Then switch to your backup. The key is the phrase "that I have ready." It signals to the audience that you anticipated this possibility and prepared for it. That single phrase transforms a failure moment into a competence signal.

**Step 2 — The Backup Demo**

Every presenter at the Helm Bank Showcase must have a recorded demo ready as a backup. This is not optional. Save it locally (not just in the cloud) and have it accessible in under fifteen seconds. Test the file format, the video playback, and the audio on the presentation room equipment before the Showcase begins.

**Step 3 — The Whiteboard Walk (if backup also fails)**

If both live and recorded demos are unavailable, fall back to storytelling with a physical or digital whiteboard. Walk the audience through your workflow step by step using a diagram you can draw in real time. This sounds catastrophic, but seasoned presenters know that a confident, articulate explanation of a workflow is often more impressive than a stumbling live demo. You know this material better than anyone in the room. Show them that.

**Step 4 — The Recovery Close**

After any failure event, close with a version of this statement:

*"What you have seen — or what I have described — represents three weeks of real work with real data in our real environment. The specific tool is a detail. The workflow, the time savings, and the results are what matter. Those are real, regardless of what happened on screen today."*

This close redirects the audience from what went wrong to what you built. It is psychologically powerful because it is true.

### Twelve Presentation Coaching Tips for AI Demos

Beyond the framework and the failure protocol, here are twelve specific coaching tips gathered from observing dozens of AI workflow presentations in banking environments.

**1. Stand to the side of the screen, not in front of it.**
Your audience needs to see the screen. When you stand in front of it, you force them to choose between watching you and watching the demo. Stand at a forty-five-degree angle to the screen, and gesture toward the relevant parts of the interface as you narrate.

**2. Narrate the why, not the what.**
The audience can see what Copilot is doing. They need you to tell them why each step matters. Instead of saying "And here I click the prompt box and type my request," say "This is the prompt I developed after seven iterations — I will show you why this specific phrasing produces better results than the more obvious approach."

**3. Use the pause strategically.**
After Copilot produces an output, pause for three full seconds before you say anything. Let the audience read it. Let the impact land. Presenters who rush past their own results are like comedians who explain the joke — they undermine the very moment they worked so hard to create.

**4. Make eye contact during the demo, not just during slides.**
Most presenters make eye contact during their slide sections and then turn to face the screen during the demo. This is backwards. The demo is your highest-impact moment. Look at your audience while Copilot is generating — watch their faces, note where they lean in, and bring them into the experience with your gaze.

**5. Translate every AI term into a banking term.**
If you use the word "prompt," immediately follow it with "which is the instruction I give Copilot." If you use the word "model," immediately follow it with "the AI engine that processes the request." Executives who are not AI practitioners will give you thirty seconds of confusion before they mentally check out. Do not make them work to follow you.

**6. Show the before and the after side by side.**
The most powerful visual in any AI workflow presentation is a side-by-side comparison of the world before Copilot and the world after. If you can show a screenshot of the old process alongside the Copilot output, the audience grasps the value immediately and viscerally.

**7. Use real numbers from real work.**
Estimated numbers are fine for projections. But your core results need to come from actual use. "I ran this workflow fourteen times over three weeks and tracked the time for each run" is infinitely more credible than "I estimate this would save about two hours." Real data earns trust.

**8. Rehearse the first ninety seconds until they are automatic.**
The first ninety seconds of your presentation are when your nervousness is highest and your audience is forming their first impression. If those ninety seconds are automatic — so practiced that you could deliver them in your sleep — your body will relax into the rest of the presentation naturally.

**9. Have your slides and your demo in the same window.**
Nothing breaks presentation flow like switching between applications. If your slides are in PowerPoint and your demo is in a browser, organize them so that switching is a single keystroke. Better yet, embed a video of your demo directly into a slide so you never leave the presentation environment.

**10. Time yourself with a countdown timer, not a clock.**
A clock shows you the time. A countdown timer shows you how much time you have left. Put a timer on a device that only you can see (not projected to the audience) and set it for ten minutes. Knowing you have four minutes left is actionable. Knowing it is 2:17 PM is not.

**11. Prepare two versions: the eight-minute version and the twelve-minute version.**
In practice, you will almost never have exactly ten minutes. Questions will interrupt early. Introductions will run long. Technical setup will eat into your slot. Know which sections of your presentation you can expand if you have extra time and which sections you can cut if you are running short. The Results and Future Vision segment is usually the easiest to compress or expand without losing coherence.

**12. End with a question, not a thank-you.**
The conventional presentation close is "Thank you for your attention. Do you have any questions?" This is weak because it hands control to the audience at the moment when your message should be landing hardest. Instead, close with a question that you choose:

*"Before I open it up for your questions, I want to ask you one: if your team got eight hundred hours back this year, what would you do with them?"*

This close is memorable, it implies confidence in your results, and it shifts the Q&A into a collaborative conversation rather than an interrogation.

## Part Four: Executive Q&A Preparation

### The Skeptic's Playbook

Banking executives who attend AI Showcases are not adversaries — but they are not cheerleaders either. They are professionals whose job is to manage risk, and their questions reflect that responsibility. The best way to prepare for executive Q&A is to spend time in the skeptic's chair before the presentation and generate the hardest questions you can imagine.

Here are the twelve most common skeptic questions from banking executives, along with model answers calibrated for the Helm Bank context.

---

**Q1: "How do we know the AI output is accurate?"**

*Model answer:* "Great question, and it is the right one to ask first. We do not rely on Copilot output without human verification — every workflow I have built includes a human review step before any output is used for a compliance or client-facing purpose. What Copilot does is extract, organize, and flag — it does not make final determinations. In our pilot, we validated every output against the source document for the first two weeks and found a 94% accuracy rate on structured field extraction, with the remaining 6% correctly flagged for manual review by the confidence indicator I built in. We are not replacing judgment — we are reducing the volume of rote work so that judgment can be applied where it actually matters."

---

**Q2: "What happens to our client data when it goes through Copilot?"**

*Model answer:* "This was my first question when I started the project, and I want to give you a precise answer. Microsoft Copilot for Microsoft 365 — which is what we use — operates entirely within Helm Bank's Microsoft 365 tenant. Client data processed through Copilot is governed by our existing Microsoft data processing agreement, which includes enterprise-grade data isolation. The data does not flow to Microsoft's training systems, and it does not leave our environment. I have documentation from Microsoft confirming this architecture if you would like to review it. Our IT and compliance teams were part of the pilot approval process."

---

**Q3: "What is the regulatory exposure here? Is this compliant with BSA/AML requirements?"**

*Model answer:* "I want to be clear about the scope of what Copilot does in this workflow. It handles data extraction and formatting — clerical work. It does not make BSA/AML determinations. Every decision that carries regulatory weight — every suspicious activity flag, every sanctions screening result, every compliance determination — remains with a licensed analyst following established protocols. Copilot is a research and preparation tool in this workflow, not a compliance decision-maker. I worked with our compliance officer during the pilot design phase to ensure the workflow design reflects that boundary explicitly."

---

**Q4: "We already have other software and existing systems. Why do we need this?"**

*Model answer:* "That is worth examining directly. Our existing systems handle their designated functions well. What they do not handle is the ad-hoc, unstructured work that falls between our formal systems — particularly the work that involves processing documents in multiple languages, extracting data from non-standardized formats, and bridging information between systems. Copilot is not competing with our core banking platform — it is filling the space between our structured workflows, where our people currently rely on manual effort, email, and institutional memory. Think of it as the connective tissue between the systems we already have."

---

**Q5: "How long did this take to build? Is it scalable?"**

*Model answer:* "The initial workflow took approximately two weeks to design and test — most of that time was in prompt development and validation, not in technical infrastructure. Because it runs entirely within Microsoft 365 tools our team already uses, there is no new software to install or license for most of the workflow. Scaling it to additional team members is primarily a training exercise — showing colleagues the workflow and the prompts. A more ambitious scale — across departments or the full bank — would require a more structured rollout, but the foundational work is done and replicable."

---

**Q6: "What if the AI is wrong and we do not catch it?"**

*Model answer:* "That is the right risk to focus on, and it is why every workflow I have designed includes explicit error detection and human checkpoints. But I would also ask us to benchmark this risk against the current baseline. The manual process we were running before had its own error rate — and those errors were often harder to detect because they did not generate a visible flag. Copilot's errors tend to be systematic and detectable: if the prompt is misconfigured, the output is visibly wrong. Human errors tend to be random and invisible until something downstream breaks. I am not arguing that AI is error-free — I am arguing that the error profile is different, and in many ways more manageable."

---

**Q7: "What is the ROI?"**

*Model answer:* "Based on our three-week pilot, I can give you three numbers. First, time savings: 83% reduction in per-document processing time, translating to approximately 1,400 analyst-hours per year at current volume. At our fully-loaded labor cost, that represents significant annual value — which I can break down in more detail after this presentation. Second, error reduction: a measurable decline in downstream correction work that I can quantify if we track it over a full quarter. Third, capacity: with those 1,400 hours recovered, our analysts can take on higher-value work — relationship support, complex case analysis — without additional headcount. The ROI case gets stronger the more volume we push through the workflow."

---

**Q8: "Is this just a fad? Will AI still be relevant in three years?"**

*Model answer:* "The specific tools will evolve — that is certain. But the underlying capability — large language models that can process and organize unstructured text — is a foundational technology, not a trend. The workflows we are building today will run on better models in three years. The prompts we are writing today will become templates for more sophisticated automation. The judgment we are developing about where AI adds value and where human expertise is essential — that judgment is durable regardless of which specific platform we use. We are not betting on Copilot staying static. We are building organizational capability to work with AI, which will be valuable as long as AI exists."

---

**Q9: "What is the implementation risk? What if we roll this out and it causes problems?"**

*Model answer:* "Every implementation carries risk, and I want to be honest about that. The risks I see are: inconsistent prompt usage across team members leading to variable outputs, over-reliance on Copilot output without adequate human review, and scope creep into use cases where Copilot is not the right tool. The mitigations are: standardized prompt libraries that define exactly how Copilot should be used for each workflow, clear documentation of the human review requirements, and a governance framework that defines which use cases are approved and which require additional review. None of those mitigations are difficult — they are discipline and documentation, which Helm Bank is already good at."

---

**Q10: "How does this affect our people? Are we replacing jobs?"**

*Model answer:* "This is the question I take most seriously, and I want to answer it directly. This workflow does not eliminate positions. It eliminates the most tedious part of existing positions. Our analysts still review every document, make every compliance determination, and manage every client relationship. What they no longer do is spend an hour and a half on Monday morning manually copying data from PDFs into spreadsheets. In every conversation I had with the team during the pilot, the reaction was relief — not fear. People went into banking because they are good with relationships and judgment, not because they love data entry. Giving them their time back for the work they actually trained for is not a threat to them. It is a gift."

---

**Q11: "Can we see the actual prompts you used?"**

*Model answer:* "Absolutely — I have them documented and I am happy to share the full prompt library after this presentation. I would also note that the prompts themselves represent a significant part of the intellectual work of this project. It took seven iterations to develop a prompt that consistently extracts all twelve required fields with the accuracy rate I described. That is institutional knowledge that should be captured and standardized, and I would recommend we do that as part of any broader rollout."

---

**Q12: "What would you need to take this to the next level?"**

*Model answer:* "Three things. First, executive sponsorship — a champion at the senior level who can open doors for cross-department expansion. Second, approximately a day or two of coordination time with IT and compliance to formalize the governance framework I have outlined informally during this pilot. Third, a small budget for formal Copilot licenses for the team members who do not currently have them — which I can justify with the ROI numbers I have shown. I am not asking for a large investment. I am asking for the organizational attention to convert a pilot into a program."

### Building Your Champion Network

A champion network is a group of colleagues across Helm Bank who share your interest in AI adoption and who actively support each other's experiments. It is the single most powerful accelerator for sustainable AI adoption at an institutional level.

Your champion network is not the same as your AI working group, though there may be overlap. A champion network is:

- **Cross-functional** — it includes people from operations, compliance, relationship banking, technology, and finance, not just your own team
- **Voluntary** — membership is driven by genuine interest, not organizational mandate
- **Action-oriented** — it does things: shares prompts, reviews workflows, celebrates wins, diagnoses failures
- **Connected to leadership** — at least one member has regular access to a senior decision-maker who can open doors

**How to start building your champion network this week:**

Step 1: Identify the five colleagues at Helm Bank who showed the most genuine curiosity about your project during the course or the pilot. These are your founding members.

Step 2: Schedule an informal forty-five-minute lunch or coffee conversation — not a meeting, not a presentation. Share what you built, listen to what problems they face, and explore whether there are workflow overlaps.

Step 3: Create a shared space for your champion network. A Microsoft Teams channel called "AI Experiments" works well. Keep it low-pressure: share interesting prompts, flag articles, celebrate small wins. The goal is a living conversation, not a formal committee.

Step 4: Commit to a monthly share-out. One hour per month where someone demonstrates something new. It does not have to be polished. It just has to be real.

The champion network serves another function that is easy to overlook: it provides psychological support. AI adoption in a traditional financial institution is not always easy. There will be skeptical managers, compliance reviews that move slowly, and technology constraints that feel arbitrary. Having colleagues who share the vision — and who check in to ask "what are you working on?" — makes the difference between persistence and abandonment.

### Measuring ROI Over Time

Your Showcase presentation included ROI estimates based on three weeks of pilot data. Those estimates are valuable for making the initial case — but they are not sufficient for securing ongoing investment or executive sponsorship.

Measuring ROI over time requires establishing a measurement protocol before you expand the workflow, not after.

**The Helm Bank AI ROI Framework:**

**Baseline Measurement — Do This Now**
Before expanding your workflow beyond the pilot, document the following for the process you automated:
- Average time per task in minutes under the old process
- Error rate or rework rate under the old process
- Volume per week or month
- Qualitative description of what the work felt like for the people doing it

**Ongoing Measurement — Do This Monthly**
For every month the workflow is running, track:
- Average time per task under the new process
- Error rate under the new process, if measurable
- Volume processed
- Any incidents or quality issues

Calculate the delta month over month. Express it in hours saved, errors avoided, and — where you can — dollars.

**Reporting Cadence**
Present your ROI data to your champion network monthly and to your direct manager quarterly. The quarterly report to your manager is the foundation of your executive sponsorship case.

One practical tip: track your data in a simple Microsoft Excel file or a shared SharePoint list from day one. The cost of capturing data is minimal when you build the habit at the start. The cost of reconstructing it six months later — when an executive asks "so how is this actually going?" — is enormous.

### Getting Executive Sponsorship at Helm Bank

Executive sponsorship is the force multiplier that transforms a personal project into an institutional initiative. Without it, your AI workflow is a clever hack that lives on your laptop. With it, it becomes a team standard, then a department standard, then potentially a bank-wide capability.

The path to executive sponsorship at Helm Bank follows a predictable sequence.

**Phase 1 — Results Credibility (Months 1 to 2 post-Showcase)**
Focus entirely on producing demonstrable results in your own area. Do not ask for anything. Just build the data. Your goal in this phase is to have numbers you can stand behind when the sponsorship conversation begins.

**Phase 2 — Manager Alignment (Month 2)**
Bring your ROI data to your direct manager. Frame it as a department opportunity, not a personal success. Ask for support to expand the workflow to your immediate team. A manager who sees their team's productivity improving is a natural advocate.

**Phase 3 — Peer Credibility (Months 2 to 3)**
Use your champion network to document at least two additional use cases beyond your original project. Each new use case broadens the argument that this is a department-wide opportunity, not a one-person experiment.

**Phase 4 — The Executive Conversation (Months 3 to 4)**
Request a thirty-minute meeting with a senior leader — ideally your manager's manager or a department head — with your manager present. Frame the conversation around business outcomes, not technology. Lead with the ROI data. Follow with the scale opportunity. Close with a specific, bounded ask.

**The Anatomy of the Sponsorship Ask:**

A strong executive sponsorship ask at Helm Bank has three elements:

1. **A specific investment:** "I am asking for your support to dedicate four hours per month of IT liaison time to help us formalize the governance framework."
2. **A specific outcome:** "In exchange, I can commit to delivering a measurable reduction in processing time across the team by the end of the next quarter."
3. **A specific accountability:** "I will report back to you monthly with the numbers, and I will flag immediately if anything falls short of the commitment."

Small, specific, accountable asks build executive trust faster than large, ambitious proposals. Once trust is established, the bigger proposals become much easier.

**A note on timing at Helm Bank specifically:** Helm Bank's position as Miami's international gateway bank, bridging the Latin America and United States corridor, creates a particular context for AI adoption conversations. The bank processes significant volumes of documents in Spanish and Portuguese, manages compliance requirements across multiple jurisdictions, and serves clients whose business cycles do not always align with US banking norms. These are exactly the conditions where AI-assisted workflows have the most dramatic impact — and the most compelling ROI story. When you make your executive sponsorship case, frame it in this context. You are not pitching an AI tool. You are pitching a capability that makes Helm Bank's unique position in the corridor more competitive, more efficient, and more resilient.

### The AI Champion Mindset

Beyond networks, metrics, and sponsorship strategies, sustaining AI momentum at Helm Bank requires a specific mindset — what we call the AI Champion Mindset.

The AI Champion Mindset has four operating principles, which map directly to Helm Bank's core values.

**Empathy First, Technology Second**
Every AI workflow you propose should begin with a genuine understanding of the human problem it addresses. Technology that solves real pain earns adoption. Technology that solves imaginary pain earns skepticism. Before you build, sit with the people who do the work and listen. Ask them: what part of your day do you most wish would disappear? What would you do with that time if you got it back? Build from their answers, not from your assumptions.

This is Helm Bank's empathy value in practice — not as a corporate value statement, but as a design discipline.

**Curiosity as a Discipline**
AI capabilities evolve faster than any training course can capture. The practitioners who remain relevant are not the ones who mastered a specific tool — they are the ones who developed the habit of curious exploration. Schedule thirty minutes per week to try something new with Copilot, even if you have no specific use case in mind. Play. Experiment. Fail cheaply. The next breakthrough often comes from a session that started with "I wonder what happens if..."

Curiosity is not a personality trait. It is a practice. Schedule it.

**Creativity in Service of Clarity**
The most creative AI workflows at Helm Bank will not be the flashiest — they will be the ones that make a complex process clear and simple for the people who use them. Design for clarity. Optimize for adoption. The workflow that ten people use consistently beats the workflow that one person uses brilliantly. When you are tempted to add complexity, ask: does this addition make it easier or harder for a colleague to pick this up and run with it?

**Passion With Patience**
AI adoption in a regulated, relationship-driven financial institution is not a sprint. It is a long game. You will encounter skeptical managers, compliance reviews that move slowly, and technology constraints that feel arbitrary. All of it is legitimate. The champion who persists — who keeps building, keeps sharing, keeps measuring — is the one who changes the institution. Passion provides the energy. Patience provides the strategy.

There is a specific kind of patience that AI champions need: the patience to let results speak before asking for investment. Every executive at Helm Bank has been promised transformative technology before. Many of those promises did not deliver. The champion who shows up with six months of clean data and a quiet confidence is more persuasive than the champion who showed up on day one with a vision deck and no evidence.

Build first. Show the results. Then ask.

### What the Next Six Months Could Look Like

To make the post-Showcase path concrete, here is a realistic arc for a Helm Bank AI champion over the first six months after the course ends.

**Month 1: Consolidate and document**
Polish your workflow documentation. Create a user guide for the prompt library you developed. Share it with two colleagues on your team. Track time savings obsessively. Keep it simple and operational.

**Month 2: First expansion**
Train one additional colleague on your workflow. Use the experience to identify gaps in your documentation and close them. Add a second use case if one presents itself naturally — do not force it.

**Month 3: Champion network launch**
Host the first monthly share-out with your champion network. Spend thirty minutes on your workflow, thirty minutes hearing about what others are experimenting with. This is the seed of a community of practice.

**Month 4: Manager report**
Prepare a one-page ROI summary — time saved, error reduction, qualitative feedback from the team. Bring it to your manager. Ask for support to expand to the full team. This is a low-stakes ask with strong data behind it.

**Month 5: Cross-department conversation**
Identify one use case in a different department that is analogous to yours. Offer to spend one hour with that team sharing your approach. Do not oversell. Just share. Let the results do the persuading.

**Month 6: Executive readiness**
You now have six months of data, two or more active use cases, a cross-department connection, and a manager who is on your side. You are ready for the executive conversation. Request the meeting. Come prepared. Make the specific, accountable ask.

Six months of consistent, evidence-based progress is more powerful than any pitch deck.
