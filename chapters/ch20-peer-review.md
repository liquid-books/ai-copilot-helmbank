## The Mirror You Cannot Hold Yourself

Six weeks ago, you walked into this program carrying a vague sense that artificial intelligence was either going to change everything at Helm Bank or turn out to be another wave of technology hype that crashed against the rocks of day-to-day banking reality. You had questions. You had skepticism. You had — if you are honest with yourself — a certain amount of defensiveness about what it might mean for your role, your expertise, and the work you have spent years mastering.

Now you are seven weeks in. You have built something. Not a concept, not a thought experiment — an actual AI-assisted workflow that addresses a real problem in your area of the bank. It may not be perfect. It probably has rough edges, unresolved questions, and sections that still feel more aspirational than operational. But it exists. It has a name, a problem statement, a Copilot integration strategy, and — if you have done the earlier assignments — at least a rough sense of what success would look like in measurable terms.

Today, you show it to your peers.

This chapter is about that experience: why structured peer review is one of the most powerful tools in any professional development program, how to use the Helm Bank Peer Review Rubric to give feedback that actually helps rather than feedback that merely sounds helpful, what common improvement patterns emerge when banking professionals review each other's AI projects, and how to walk out of this session with a genuinely sharper version of your work.

We will also pause midway through for a reflection exercise that matters more than it might appear. Because one of the most important things this session can reveal is not just what is wrong with your project — it is how differently you now think about your work than you did on Day 1.

## Why Peer Review Works in Professional Settings

There is a reason that every serious design discipline — architecture, engineering, medicine, software development — has built peer review into its professional culture. It is not primarily about catching errors, though it does that. It is about something more fundamental: **the gap between the map and the territory.**

When you have been living inside a project for weeks, your mental model of it becomes so detailed, so richly elaborated in your own mind, that you start to mistake the map for the territory. You know what you meant when you wrote "streamline the client onboarding review process." You know which specific bottlenecks you were thinking about, which compliance requirements create friction, which team members are involved and why. None of that context lives on the page. It lives in your head.

Your reviewers only have the page. And that gap — between what you intended and what you communicated — is exactly where projects go wrong.

Peer review forces that gap into the open. When a colleague says "I am not sure what the actual trigger for Copilot is in step three," they are not being dense. They are showing you the fissure in your thinking that you could not see because you were standing too close.

This is especially true for AI projects. Copilot workflows involve an unusual combination of technical assumptions (what Copilot can and cannot do), process assumptions (how the workflow actually unfolds in practice), and outcome assumptions (what counts as a meaningful improvement). Any one of these can harbor invisible assumptions that your peers — coming from different roles, different departments, different levels of AI experience — will surface almost immediately.

### Helm Bank's Specific Stakes

At Helm Bank, peer review carries additional weight because of the nature of the institution. You are an international bank operating at the intersection of US regulatory requirements and Latin American business relationships. The stakes of getting an AI workflow wrong are not just inefficiency — they can mean compliance failures, client relationship damage, or reputational harm in markets where trust is the fundamental currency.

This means your peers are not just giving you aesthetic feedback. They are, in a sense, doing a professional risk review. When a colleague in Compliance says "I do not see how this handles the OFAC screening step," that is not a stylistic observation. It is a flag that could save you from a serious mistake. When a colleague in Relationship Management says "our priority clients in Brazil would never respond well to this communication format," that is decades of cross-cultural expertise being applied to your project in real time.

Treat that expertise with the respect it deserves. Your reviewers are resources, not judges.

## Setting the Stage: The Peer Review Session Structure

Today's session runs in three phases. Understanding the structure before you begin will help you use the time well.

### Phase 1: Small Group Presentations (60 minutes)

You have been pre-assigned to a peer review group of three to four participants from different departments. Each presenter gets **twelve minutes**: eight minutes to present, four minutes for clarifying questions. This is not a lot of time. That is intentional. The constraint forces clarity.

During the eight-minute presentation, you should cover:

1. **The problem** — What specific pain point does your project address? Who experiences it? How often? What does it cost in time, accuracy, or client experience?
2. **The Copilot integration** — Where exactly does Microsoft Copilot enter your workflow? What do you prompt it to do? What does a good output look like?
3. **The before/after story** — What does the work look like today, without AI? What does it look like with your proposed workflow? Be concrete and specific.
4. **The measurable impact** — What would you measure to know this is working? What numbers, timelines, or quality indicators would tell the story?
5. **Where you need help** — What is the one thing your peer reviewers should focus on? Where do you feel least confident?

During the four-minute question period, reviewers ask only **clarifying questions** — questions whose purpose is to understand, not to evaluate. "Can you say more about what happens when the client is a new relationship rather than an existing one?" is a clarifying question. "Do you not think this is too complicated?" is not. Evaluation comes later.

### Phase 2: Structured Rubric Review (45 minutes)

After all presentations in your group are complete, each reviewer completes the Helm Bank Peer Review Rubric for each project they heard. We will cover the rubric in detail in the next section. This is done in writing, privately, before the group discussion begins. The writing step is essential — it prevents the phenomenon where whoever speaks first in the room shapes everyone else's feedback.

### Phase 3: Feedback Conversations (45 minutes)

The group reconvenes for feedback conversations. Each project gets approximately ten to twelve minutes of structured discussion. The presenting participant listens more than they speak during this phase. Their job is to take notes, ask follow-up clarifying questions, and resist the urge to defend.

This last point deserves emphasis. **Do not defend your project during the feedback conversation.** Not because your instinct to defend is wrong — you built this thing, you care about it, of course you want to explain why certain choices make sense. But defending closes down information flow. Every time you explain away a concern, you lose the signal that concern was carrying. Receive first. Evaluate later. Revise based on what actually holds up.

## The Helm Bank Peer Review Rubric

The rubric is the backbone of this session. It transforms peer feedback from a vague conversation into a structured professional assessment. It also protects against the natural human tendency to give positive, conflict-avoiding feedback that feels supportive but leaves the project unchanged.

Each dimension is scored on a scale of **1 to 5**, where:

- **5** = Exemplary — ready for showcase as-is, a model for others
- **4** = Strong — minor refinements would make this excellent
- **3** = Developing — solid foundation with one or two significant gaps to address
- **2** = Emerging — promising idea but requires substantial rework before showcase
- **1** = Early Stage — needs fundamental rethinking of the approach

```{list-table} Helm Bank Peer Review Rubric
:header-rows: 1
:name: tbl-peer-review-rubric
:widths: 18 35 35 12

* - Dimension
  - What to Look For (High Score)
  - Common Weaknesses (Low Score)
  - Weight

* - **1. Clarity of Problem**
  - The problem is specific, bounded, and felt by a real population at Helm Bank. A colleague unfamiliar with the department can immediately understand what pain point is being addressed, who experiences it, and why it matters. Quantitative context is provided (frequency, volume, time cost).
  - The problem is described in vague terms ("improve efficiency," "enhance client communication"). The scope is unclear — is this about one team or the whole bank? No baseline data or estimate of current pain is provided. A reviewer cannot tell whether this problem is significant or minor.
  - 20%

* - **2. Copilot Integration Quality**
  - The workflow shows a clear, specific point of Copilot entry. At least one example prompt is provided with enough detail that a peer could run it. The output from Copilot is defined — what does a "good" Copilot response look like? Human review and oversight steps are explicitly identified. The integration respects Helm Bank's data security guidelines.
  - Copilot's role is described generically ("I will use Copilot to help with the writing"). No example prompts are shown. It is unclear what Copilot actually generates versus what the human does. No mention of how outputs are reviewed for accuracy or compliance. Data sensitivity concerns are not addressed.
  - 25%

* - **3. Measurable Impact**
  - The project articulates specific, realistic metrics that would indicate success. These could be time savings (minutes per task, tasks per week), quality indicators (error rates, revision cycles), or business outcomes (client response time, compliance documentation completeness). The measurement approach is practical — data already exists or can be collected without heroic effort.
  - Outcomes are described subjectively ("better," "faster," "more consistent"). No specific metrics are identified. The measurement approach requires data that does not exist. The claimed impact seems implausibly large or disappointingly small relative to the workflow scope.
  - 20%

* - **4. Feasibility**
  - The project can be piloted within Helm Bank's current technology stack (Microsoft 365 and Copilot licenses in place). Implementation does not require IT infrastructure changes or new vendor agreements. The workflow fits within existing team capacity — it does not assume a full-time implementation resource. Regulatory and compliance considerations have been acknowledged and either addressed or flagged for next steps.
  - The project requires tools or integrations not currently available. Implementation seems to assume significant IT development work. The workflow describes an ideal-state scenario disconnected from current operational reality. Compliance or data governance concerns are not mentioned despite obvious relevance.
  - 20%

* - **5. Presentation Quality**
  - The before/after story is vivid and specific enough that a reviewer can picture both the current pain and the improved future state. The presentation uses concrete examples rather than abstract descriptions. The presenter's framing creates a sense of genuine importance — the stakes are clear. The presentation fits within the time limit.
  - The before state is described too briefly to feel real. The after state sounds like a marketing promise rather than an operational description. Examples are generic or hypothetical rather than drawn from actual Helm Bank experience. The presentation ran over time or felt rushed, suggesting the project scope has not been properly bounded.
  - 15%
```

### Using the Rubric Well

A few principles for applying the rubric effectively:

**Be specific in your comments.** A score of 3 on Copilot Integration Quality means almost nothing to the presenter without an explanation. "I scored this a 3 because the prompt you showed for generating the credit memo is specific enough, but I could not find any mention of how the output gets reviewed before it goes into the client file — who does that review, and what are they checking for?" That is a useful comment. "Copilot integration could be clearer" is not.

**Let your scores speak.** If you feel social pressure to soften your written feedback because you like the presenter or do not want to create conflict, remember: a polite 4 that should be a 2 is not kindness. It is the opposite of kindness. It sends someone into the final showcase with a project that has fundamental problems. Give the score the work deserves, then use your written comments to show you are on the presenter's side.

**Notice what you are reacting to.** If you find yourself wanting to give a low score on Feasibility because you personally think AI is moving too fast, check whether that is about the project or about your own discomfort. The rubric is about the project, not your priors. Similarly, if you want to give a high score on everything because you are impressed by how polished the presenter seems, slow down. Scores should reflect the work.

**Use the weight column.** The five dimensions are not equally important. Copilot Integration Quality (25%) matters more than Presentation Quality (15%). A project that scores brilliantly on presentation but has a vague or technically implausible Copilot integration is a project that needs substantial work, regardless of how impressive the slides are.

## What Good Feedback Sounds Like: Example Conversations

One of the most useful things this chapter can do is show you what effective peer feedback actually sounds like in practice — and contrast it with the kinds of feedback that feel helpful but accomplish nothing. The following examples are drawn from composite scenarios based on typical project types at financial institutions of Helm Bank's scale and complexity.

### Scenario A: The Regulatory Reporting Project

**Project summary:** A member of the Compliance team has built a Copilot-assisted workflow for generating first-draft quarterly regulatory reports. The workflow uses Copilot to summarize transaction data from Excel into narrative paragraphs that meet the format requirements of specific regulatory filings.

---

**Ineffective feedback — what to avoid:**

> "This is really impressive. I love that you are tackling compliance — that is such an important area. I think the workflow makes sense and I can see how it would save a lot of time. My only thought is maybe you could add more detail in some places? But overall, great job."

Why this fails: It says almost nothing actionable. The presenter walks away feeling validated but with no specific guidance for improvement. The reviewer has essentially abdicated their responsibility.

---

**Effective feedback:**

> "I scored your Copilot Integration Quality a 3, and here is my reasoning. The prompt you showed for summarizing transaction volumes is solid — I can see exactly what you are asking Copilot to generate. But I noticed you did not show what happens after Copilot produces the draft. Regulatory filings have very specific formatting and citation requirements, and I am wondering: does Copilot's output ever include numbers or characterizations that look plausible but are not quite right? And if so, who catches that before it goes into the filing?
>
> The reason I am raising this is not to question the concept — I think the time savings potential is real and your problem statement is excellent. But a regulatory report has essentially zero tolerance for inaccuracies, and right now the workflow seems to end at 'Copilot generates the draft.' Adding an explicit human review checklist — perhaps five specific things a reviewer should verify before approving the Copilot output — would take this from a 3 to a 5 on that dimension and make the project much more showcase-ready."

Why this works: It is specific about what the score is and why. It identifies a genuine risk — accuracy in regulatory filings — without being alarmist. It gives a concrete improvement suggestion. It frames the feedback as being in service of the presenter's success, not as a criticism of their effort.

---

### Scenario B: The Client Communication Project

**Project summary:** A relationship manager has built a workflow for using Copilot to personalize client communication templates for high-net-worth clients in the bank's Latin American corridor. The workflow takes a standard quarterly portfolio summary template and uses Copilot to adapt the language, examples, and framing based on a client profile brief.

---

**Ineffective feedback — what to avoid:**

> "I am a little concerned about this. Personalized AI-generated communication feels risky to me. What if the client finds out their letter was written by AI? And how do you control the tone? I think this needs a lot more work before you can present it to senior leadership. Honestly, I am not sure AI is the right tool for this kind of relationship-sensitive communication."

Why this fails: It raises concerns without specificity or constructive direction. The phrase "I am not sure AI is the right tool" is a personal opinion masquerading as feedback — it does not engage with the actual project design. The tone is discouraging rather than developmental. The presenter is left feeling criticized rather than helped.

---

**Effective feedback:**

> "I scored your Measurable Impact a 2, which I want to explain carefully because I think the underlying idea is strong. The problem you identified — that clients in the Monterrey and Bogotá markets respond poorly to templated communications that feel generic — is real and well-articulated. But when I look at how you plan to measure success, you have listed 'improved client satisfaction' as the outcome. That is not wrong, but it is not specific enough to be measurable. How would you know in six months whether the personalized Copilot-generated letters actually improved anything?
>
> Some suggestions: Do you have client survey data you could compare before and after? Do you track email response rates or meeting acceptance rates from client communications? Even something like tracking how many clients call or email back after receiving their quarterly summary would give you a signal. Picking one of those and setting a realistic target — 'we expect response rates to increase from 12 percent to 18 percent over two quarters' — would take this dimension from a 2 to a 4.
>
> The other thing I would add: your before/after story is genuinely compelling. The sample letter you showed for the generic template versus the personalized version was a great illustration. Consider leading with that comparison in your showcase presentation — it makes the impact feel tangible immediately."

Why this works: The low score is explained clearly and non-defensively. It identifies the specific missing element. It offers concrete alternative approaches. It also highlights a genuine strength, which is honest and prevents the presenter from feeling the feedback is purely negative.

---

### Scenario C: The Loan Documentation Project

**Project summary:** A commercial lending analyst has built a workflow where Copilot assists in reviewing loan documentation packages for completeness, flagging missing items against a standard checklist before the file goes to underwriting.

---

**Effective feedback — example of a high-scoring project:**

> "I scored this project a 4 overall, and I want to explain why this is strong work while also being honest about the one gap I see.
>
> The problem statement is excellent — you have quantified that documentation incompleteness causes an average 2.3-day delay per loan file, and you cited actual data from the last two quarters. That is a 5. Your Copilot integration is the most technically specific of any project I reviewed today: you showed the exact prompt structure, the specific document types you are checking against, and a real example of the output with a flagged missing item highlighted. I gave that a 4 rather than a 5 because I noticed the workflow assumes Copilot has access to the file package, and I want to make sure you have confirmed with IT that sharing loan documentation folders with Copilot does not conflict with our data governance policies. That is not a conceptual problem — it might just be a permissions question — but it needs to be explicitly addressed before the showcase.
>
> Your feasibility score is a 4. The workflow fits within our Microsoft 365 environment, it does not require new tools, and the implementation plan you described is genuinely phased in a realistic way. The one thing I flagged is that you mentioned 'the team would need some training' without specifying what that training would involve or how long it would take.
>
> To get this to a 5 overall: address the data governance question explicitly, add two to three sentences about the training component, and your showcase presentation will be very strong."

Why this works: The feedback is comprehensive and dimension-by-dimension, which mirrors the rubric structure and makes it immediately usable. The distinction between "this is a conceptual problem" and "this might just be a permissions question" conveys that the issue is solvable without dismissing it. The closing paragraph gives the presenter a clear, actionable checklist.

## Common Improvement Patterns Across Projects

After running this peer review exercise with professionals from dozens of financial institutions and corporate environments, certain patterns of needed improvement appear with remarkable consistency. Being aware of them before you receive your feedback helps you receive it with more equanimity — because you will realize that the weaknesses your reviewers are identifying are not unique to you. They are the predictable artifacts of how thoughtful professionals build AI workflows in isolation.

### Pattern 1: The Vanishing Human

The most common gap in first-draft AI workflows is the absence of explicit human review steps. Presenters describe what Copilot generates but leave the transition from "AI output" to "professional deliverable" implicit. The assumption seems to be that a human will "obviously" review it — but when and how and what they are looking for is left undefined.

In banking, this gap is particularly problematic because regulatory environments, client relationship stakes, and risk management requirements all demand explicit documentation of how AI-generated content is verified before use. Your peer reviewers will flag this almost every time.

**The fix:** For every Copilot output in your workflow, add a review step with a brief checklist. What are the three to five things a human reviewer must verify? How is their approval documented?

### Pattern 2: The Aspirational Metric

Participants frequently identify outcomes they hope their project will produce without identifying how those outcomes would actually be measured. "Faster turnaround times," "better client experience," "more consistent documentation" — these are aspirations, not metrics. And they are often framed as improvements without establishing what the current baseline is.

**The fix:** For each outcome, answer three questions: What is the specific number or indicator you would measure? What is the current baseline? What is a realistic target improvement over what time period?

### Pattern 3: The Invisible Prompt

Many presenters describe Copilot's role in abstract terms without showing an actual example prompt. "Copilot will summarize the client meeting notes" tells a reviewer almost nothing about whether the workflow will work as described. A poorly constructed prompt will produce inconsistent or unreliable outputs no matter how good the surrounding workflow is.

**The fix:** Include at least one fully-written example prompt in your project documentation. Format it as you would actually type it — or paste it — into Copilot. If you have not tested the prompt yet, test it before the showcase. Show the actual output alongside the prompt.

### Pattern 4: The Scope Creep Problem (in Both Directions)

Some projects try to do too much — they encompass an entire department's AI transformation strategy when they would be better served by focusing on one specific workflow with clear boundaries. Other projects err in the opposite direction, describing a Copilot use so narrow and simple that it does not constitute a meaningful workflow worth showcasing.

**The fix:** A good project scope can be described in one sentence that specifies the trigger (what starts the workflow), the Copilot action (what Copilot specifically does), and the output (what the professional then uses). If your one-sentence description requires significant qualification or asterisks, the scope needs adjustment.

### Pattern 5: The Context Gap

Projects frequently describe workflows that make complete sense to someone who works in that department but are incomprehensible to an outsider. This matters for two reasons: first, the showcase audience will include senior leaders and colleagues who do not know your department's specific language; second, a workflow you cannot explain simply may have implicit complexity that makes it harder to implement than you realize.

**The fix:** Ask a reviewer from outside your department whether they can follow the workflow from start to finish. If they cannot, the documentation needs clarification. This is also good practice for your showcase presentation — you should be able to explain your project to someone who has no background in your functional area.

You might wonder why a reflection exercise appears in the middle of a chapter about peer review and project refinement. The answer is strategic.

Your reflection exercise gives you the raw material for a narrative arc that is far more powerful than a slide deck. The best showcase presentations follow a pattern:

> "On Day 1, I thought... and I was skeptical because... But when I saw... I started to understand that... So I built... And here is what it can do... And here is how I would measure whether it is working."

That narrative arc — from skeptic to practitioner, from vague sense of "AI is coming" to specific workflow with measurable outcomes — is what makes a showcase presentation genuinely memorable. Your reflection exercise is where you find the beginning of that story.

## Refining Your Prompts Based on Peer Input

One of the most common and most useful pieces of feedback you will receive today relates to your Copilot prompts. Prompt quality is often the invisible variable that determines whether an AI workflow succeeds or fails in practice. And because prompting is a relatively new skill, most first-draft prompts — even from thoughtful, experienced professionals — have one or more of the following weaknesses.

### Weakness 1: The Underspecified Context

First-draft prompts often assume Copilot has context it does not have. A prompt that reads "Summarize this client meeting and identify action items" assumes Copilot knows what kind of meeting it was, who the client is, what the relevant relationship history looks like, and what format the summary should take. None of that is in the prompt.

**Refined version:**
> "The following are notes from a 45-minute portfolio review meeting with an existing private banking client. The client has been with Helm Bank for 8 years and primarily holds fixed-income instruments. Please summarize the meeting in three sections: (1) Key topics discussed, (2) Client concerns or questions raised, (3) Action items with suggested owners and suggested deadlines. Format the summary as a professional internal memo of no more than 300 words."

Notice how much more that prompt tells Copilot about who it is serving, what format it is working with, what sections to produce, and what length constraints apply. When your peer reviewer says "the prompt needs more context," this is almost always what they mean.

### Weakness 2: The Unconstrained Output

Prompts that do not specify output format, length, or structure produce outputs that vary wildly in length, tone, and usefulness. This inconsistency makes the workflow hard to operationalize — if each output looks different, the human reviewer cannot develop a reliable review process.

**The fix:** Always include in your prompt the desired format (list, memo, table, paragraph), the approximate length (word count or number of items), and the intended audience or use case (internal review, client delivery, regulatory filing). These constraints dramatically increase output consistency.

### Weakness 3: The Missing Quality Signal

Effective prompts often include an example of what a good output looks like, or at minimum a description of the quality criteria the output should meet. Without this, Copilot optimizes for the most statistically common interpretation of your request — which may not match your professional standards at all.

For banking applications where quality standards are non-negotiable — regulatory language, client communication tone, risk characterization — including quality criteria in the prompt is not optional. It is part of the workflow design.

**Example addition to a prompt:**
> "The tone should be professional and client-facing — avoid internal banking jargon. Do not make specific investment recommendations or reference specific performance figures unless they are explicitly provided in the source material. The communication should feel personalized and relationship-oriented, not template-like."

### Weakness 4: The Monolithic Prompt

Complex tasks often tempt first-time AI workflow designers to write one enormous prompt that tries to do everything at once. "Analyze this loan application, identify the key risk factors, compare them to our standard underwriting criteria, draft a summary for the credit committee, and flag any compliance concerns" is four distinct tasks, and a single prompt will typically produce output that is mediocre at all of them.

**The fix:** Break complex workflows into stages. Use Copilot for one specific task per prompt, review the output, then use that output as input for the next Copilot step. This "prompt chaining" approach produces dramatically better results and gives human reviewers natural checkpoint moments throughout the workflow.

### Applying Peer Feedback to Prompt Revision

When your reviewers give you feedback on your Copilot integration, the most useful follow-up question you can ask is: "If you were going to rewrite my prompt to address that concern, what would you change?" This question invites your reviewer to be concrete and moves the conversation from problem-identification to solution-generation.

Not every reviewer will have a specific answer — they may know something is off without knowing exactly how to fix it. That is still valuable. It tells you the direction of the problem. Your job is to take that directional signal and experiment with specific prompt revisions until the output reliably matches your professional standards.

Keep a log of your prompt revisions. Note what you changed, what the output looked like before and after, and what the difference was in quality or consistency. This log is not just good practice — it is documentation that demonstrates the rigor of your workflow design, which is exactly the kind of evidence that makes a showcase presentation credible.

## Sharpening Your Before/After Story

The before/after story is the narrative engine of your showcase presentation. It is the reason someone in the audience will lean forward rather than check their phone. And it is almost always underdeveloped in first-draft presentations.

Here is a diagnostic test: read your current before state description and ask yourself, "Would someone who has never done this job understand why this is painful?" If the answer is no — if the pain only makes sense to someone already immersed in your department's processes — the before story needs more work.

### Making the Before State Real

The most effective before stories use one of three techniques:

**The Time Calculation.** Quantify the current process in human minutes or hours. "Every quarterly compliance report requires a compliance analyst to spend approximately four hours manually cross-referencing transaction logs with regulatory requirements. With a team of three analysts and twelve reports per year, that is 144 hours annually — the equivalent of more than three and a half full work weeks dedicated to a process that is largely mechanical and error-prone."

Numbers make the pain real. They also make the after state legible — if you save 50 percent of that time, the value is immediately tangible.

**The Specific Moment.** Describe one specific instance of the problem as a narrative. "Imagine it is the last Friday before a reporting deadline. An analyst is manually copying figures from four different spreadsheets into a Word document, checking each figure against the regulatory format guide, and flagging inconsistencies by hand. She has been doing this for three hours. She finds an error in figure 23 that requires her to go back to source data and recalculate. She fixes it, but the error propagates downstream to figures 31 and 47, which she does not catch until the compliance officer reviews the draft on Monday morning."

This kind of narrative makes the before state viscerally real. It also sets up the after state beautifully — because the audience can immediately see where Copilot could have intervened.

**The Comparison.** Show the before state alongside the after state simultaneously. A side-by-side visual — the current manual process flowchart versus the AI-assisted workflow — can communicate in ten seconds what would take a minute of verbal explanation. This technique is especially effective for audiences who are visually oriented or who may not have time to absorb a long narrative.

### Making the After State Credible

After stories often fail for the opposite reason that before stories fail: they are too optimistic. They describe an ideal-state outcome that requires everything to go right, all the time, without acknowledging the realistic rough edges.

Paradoxically, a more modest, credible after story is often more persuasive than an ambitious one. Saying "this workflow will reduce documentation review time by 40 percent in the first six months, with the expectation of further optimization as the team becomes more proficient" is more convincing than "this will transform our loan processing entirely." It shows that you understand implementation is a process, not a switch.

The most effective after stories also show the human in the new workflow, not just the AI. Show what the analyst does differently. Show the review checklist they use. Show the moment where human judgment is essential and explicitly reserved for the human. This is not just good communication — it is accurate. Copilot is a tool that augments human professionals, not a replacement for them. The after story should make that abundantly clear.

## After the Session: Your Revision Checklist

Before you leave the peer review session today, organize the feedback you received into a simple revision checklist. The following structure will help you convert peer input into concrete action items rather than vague intentions.

```{admonition} Post-Review Revision Checklist
:class: important

For each item of feedback you received, categorize it:

**Category A — Fix Before Showcase (Must Do)**
These are gaps that, if left unaddressed, will undermine the credibility or usefulness of your project. Typically: missing human review steps, unspecified metrics, unaddressed compliance considerations, or prompts that have not been tested.

**Category B — Sharpen for Showcase (Should Do)**
These are improvements that would make your project more compelling or clearer but would not fundamentally change its credibility. Typically: stronger before/after story, more specific example prompts, cleaner visual presentation, tighter scope description.

**Category C — Consider for Future Phases (Nice to Have)**
These are good ideas that go beyond the scope of the current showcase project. Note them, but do not let them expand your scope at this late stage. They belong in your post-showcase implementation roadmap.

For each Category A and B item, write:
- The specific change you will make
- What resource or information you need to make it
- By when you will complete it (the showcase is in two weeks)
```

The revision checklist is not just an organizational tool. It is a commitment device. When you write down "I will add a human review checklist to Step 4 of the documentation workflow by next Thursday," you have made a specific, time-bound commitment that is far more likely to get done than a vague intention to "improve the review process."

Share your Category A checklist with at least one peer reviewer before you leave today. Ask them to check in with you in one week to confirm you have addressed the items. Accountability partnerships — even informal ones — dramatically increase follow-through on post-feedback revisions.

## A Note on Receiving Feedback Gracefully

We have talked at length about how to give effective feedback. It is worth ending this chapter with a few words about how to receive it.

The experience of presenting work you care about and hearing it critiqued — even constructively, even by colleagues who are clearly trying to help — is not easy. It triggers a defensive response in most people, and that response is entirely human and entirely understandable. You built something. You thought about it carefully. You know why you made the choices you made.

Here are three practices that experienced professionals use to receive feedback productively:

**Write everything down.** Even feedback you immediately disagree with. Especially feedback you immediately disagree with. The disagreement might be right — but it might also be the defensive response talking. Write it down, sit with it for a day, and evaluate it with fresh eyes. You will often find that the feedback you most wanted to dismiss was carrying the most important signal.

**Separate the feedback from the relationship.** Peer feedback is not a judgment of you as a professional or a person. It is an assessment of a work product at a specific stage of development. The colleague who scored your Copilot integration a 2 is not saying you are bad at your job. They are saying that section of your project needs more work. Those are profoundly different statements.

**Ask "what would have to be true for this feedback to be right?"** This is a technique borrowed from strategic consulting. When you receive a piece of feedback that you are inclined to dismiss, try to genuinely engage with the scenario in which the reviewer is correct. What would the world look like? What would have to be true about your workflow or your assumptions? Sometimes you will conclude the reviewer is mistaken — and you will now have a clear argument for why. More often, you will find the kernel of truth that your defensive response was trying to protect you from seeing.

There is a deeper reason to receive feedback gracefully that goes beyond this program. The professionals at Helm Bank who will have the most impact as AI becomes embedded in banking operations are not necessarily the ones who are most technically adept with AI tools. They are the ones who can learn quickly, who can take honest information about their work and use it to improve, and who can collaborate effectively with colleagues who have different expertise and perspectives. Today's peer review session is practice for that capacity — and it matters long after the showcase is over.
